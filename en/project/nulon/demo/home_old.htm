Definition: NULO is a top-level ontology or foundation ontology. It includes a list of very general terms that are the same across more specific knowledge domains. It is similar to a thesaurus because it groups together these terms in categories according to similarity of meaning and it is like a dictionary because it can also include multiple, multi-lingual definitions. It is an ontology because it defines relations that describe how the terms can be linked together or linked to web information resources and because it defines general properties for each item for management purposes. The structure of NULO is best represented with an undirected graph.

Abstract

NULO is based on topic maps standard for information interchange, everything is represented by a Topic. Topic can be both a container and a handle for everything, including abstract concepts, web resources, physical objects, and others. NULO adopts and advocates the use of a Topic as the core mechanism of the next generation of Internet, i.e. Web 3.0 or GGG (Global Giant Graph). In NULO Topic is synonymous with the term "Information Resource" (IR). NULO makes an explicit distinction between Binary Information Resources (BIRs) and Term Information Resources (TIRs).

The concept map you see on the left side, has definitions for all these terms as well as how these are related to other terms that are used in the topic map data model.

Part of the NULO effort is an attempt to define the core ontology of Web 3.0 in a practical, well defined, standardized and consistent way so that it can be shared and used among those users that generate or use ontologies. NULO is different in many aspects from other similar upper ontologies. Based on the topic map standard, it is very easy to extend with definitions from other languages and ontologies, and it includes a minimal set of elements, categorized and abbreviated for memorizing. Secondly TIR to BIR, BIR to BIR, and TIR to TIR relations are defined at a logical layer and instances of these relations are asserted. NULO follows the topic map standard and allows the definition of n-ary, undirected relations where the arguments play a specific role.

Last but not least, NULO ontology is a system of ontological categories defined in such a way to alleviate the work of the information architect. One of these categories deals with the abstraction principles, hierarchy, taxonomy, meronomy, grouping, faceting, and instantiation and how these are modeled and used in topic maps. Another category includes terms like identifiedBy, subjectOf, isAbout, refersTo, that are used for mapping BIRs to TIRs, i.e. subject indexing. The combination of the two in NULO topic maps makes a well defined and intuitive method for searching and retrieving information resources.

Information Resource vs Information


Information resource is the central term of information science. It is the corner stone of modelling for any information system that manages data. This term has caused so much confusion among scientists, take for example the identity crisis problem that for many is still an issue. Even the definition of the term in W3C architecture is heavily dependent on other terms such as information, resource, representation, data encoding, essential characteristics, and message. This blending of unclear semantics and muddled statements leads to naive conclusions such as "a  dog is a resource". But what kind of resource a dog is? On the contrary, in NULO information resource is always representing "Information". Therefore an information resource is considered to be a representation of information.

Information describes fully some entity, a concept, an object, a subject, a being, an idea, therefore an information resource can be a representation for these terms. We can only approach anything that can be conceived or perceived from a certain perspective, in a subjective way and in a very limited sense, because of our communication obstacles. It is only part of information we have access to depending on our intelligence and spiritual endeavor.

In W3C the term "resource" is associated with the same notion as that of the term "information", in Greek "πληροφορία", see the concept map on the left side. In section 2.2. URI/Resource Relationships we read, "although it is possible to describe a great many things about a car or a dog in a sequence of bits, the sum of those things will invariably be an approximation of the essential character of the resource".

The reader should have already been in trouble absorbing the definitions and relations among these terms. Perhaps the easiest way to obtain an accurate perspective will be to think in terms of a reference. From the W3C perspective, a resource is the referent of an information resource. From the NULO perspective, information refers to an entity, a concept, an object, a subject, a being, an idea, or a resource? Information is an abstract thing, it can be something that is conceived in the mind, or simply something preexisting. Information is something that does not have form. This is contrary to the 15th century latin loan definition of the term "Information" in Wikipedia. When we start discussing the form of information, then we are entering the world of information resources and the various ways these are realized by humans. Information resources represent information not wholly but partially, i.e. some aspect of information.

Hopefully the Unified Information Representation in Four Levels (Data - Information Resources - Information - Entities) diagram, a concept map designed especially for clarifying the basic concepts behind NULO, will help you see things from a different perspective and understand why we need to keep separate the addressing mechanism, i.e. URL, from identity, the content from the container and the content format, the description of a "resource" from the description of "information", the encoding/decoding at a human level from the same process simulated on a machine.

Key Concepts Illustration

We have eight different core concepts, to deal with in NULO :
  1. Information Resource (TMAP-IR) 
  2. Term Information Resource (TMAP-TIR)
  3. Binary Information Resource (TMAP-BIR)
  4. Association (TMAP-ASSOC)
  5. Role (TMAP-R)
  6. Occurrence (TMAP-OCC)
  7. Variable (TMAP-VAR)
  8. Scope (TMAP-SCO) 

  • We extended the term "Information Resource" in topic maps to include non-binary representations. TMAP-IR in turn is divided into two broad categories, the TMAP-TIR and the TMAP-BIR.
  • Term Information Resource (TIR) shares the same definition as the "subject indicator" in topic map terminology. They are described by words, compound words, and phrases that in specific contexts are given specific meaning. TIRs in NULO are defined by using mainly Wikipedia and OpenCyc controlled vocabularies. NULO is aiming to promote consistent usage of these terms in communities of users. TIRs use URL references that start with the '@' symbol. They are called term identifiers or subject identifiers as they are known in the data model of Topic Map. To distinguish the notion of the term from that of BIR one has to think in terms of the container of information. For example natural objects, e.g. tree, animal, has a non-digital container. This is a candidate for a TIR. On the other hand an electronic document in the form of a computer file is included in a digital container, therefore it is a case of a BIR.
An example in Linear Topic Map notation:
[AHatzis = "Athanassios I. Hatzis" @"http://athanassios.gr"]

Topic with ID=AHatzis represents a person.

  • Binary Information Resources (BIRs) represent web resources, computer files and folders, web or database services, anything that appears to be in a digital format and that can be retrieved with an application protocol such as http, file, smtp, or ftp. In fact that is the definition of information resource given in the topic map data model. A BIR is identified with a URL that starts with the '%' symbol. This reference is called "subject locator" according to the Topic Map data model. This way URL serves its initial purpose, to locate the binary information resource by describing its primary access mechanism.

An example in Linear Topic Map notation:

[athanassios.gr = "athanassios.gr" %"http://athanassios.gr"]

Topic with ID=athanassios.gr represents a web page. It is a personal web page of that person.


More examples:

[Microsoft-Access = "The Microsoft Access Relational Database" @"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Access"]

[myCarDB = "My personal Car database built in Microsoft Access" %"file://home/athanassios/cars.accdb"]

 

[EDI  ="Electronic_data_interchange" @"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_data_interchange"]

[EDI_Tutorial ="EDI Tutorial 1: Before You Begin the Tutorial" %"http://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/biztalk/gg153503.aspx"]

  • A topic may represent an n-ary relation, in Topic Maps terminology it is named association, among other topics. For example, a quarternary relation (AGNT-act-annotate) that takes four arguments (AHatzis,comment123,Wikipedia,var145)

Example in Linear Topic Map notation:

AGNT-act-annotate  (AHatzis :AGNT-generic,
                                comment123 :INF-CONT-annotation,
                                Wikipedia :TMAP-Topic,
                                var145 :INF-SDATA-time)

  • Topic may represent a role in the n-ary relation.

Example :

AGNT-generic, INF-CONT-annotation, TMAP-Topic, and INF-SDATA-time are the roles that the four arguments of the AGNT-act-annotate play
 
  • A Topic may also represent an attribute that can be linked to other topics as occurrence type or as an association argument. In the first scenario, this is the well-known EAV(entity, attribute, value) model. Attributes in topic maps terminology are called occurrences. It is like you define a special type of association between the topic and the attribute. The value of the attribute is  maintained internally and it is not exposed to other topics. A rule of a thumb is to use occurrence when you want to link a piece of data, an attribute, to a Topic that is not dependent from other Topics. For example, we can use occurrences to relate time stamps for events, e.g. create, modify.

Example:

{TopicX, INF-CONT-created_on, "11/8/2012 8:11:56 pm"} /INF-SDATA-dateTime
 

In the second case we use attributes as arguments in a relation. A typical example of a sentence model can be the following: "Athanassios was 1m high at the age of four". In NULO we introduce the Variable topic as an enhancement to the existing Topic Map data model. Be aware of the distinction between attributes (occurrences) and relations (associations) because this is another great cause of semantic confusion. In LTM notation we have the following ascertion:

hasProperty(Athanassios:Agent, varinst223:Height, varinst554:Age)

Where varinst223 is of type Topic-VAR and takes a value e.g. "was 1m high".

Where varinst553 is of type Topic-VAR and takes a value e.g. "at the age of four"

  • Finally a rather difficult concept to grasp in topic maps that is not related to any usual concept in other software data models is that of scope.  A topic may represent a scope e.g. a natural language or a definition according to some other ontology. 
Example :

{AHatzis, NAM-H-first, "Αθανάσιος"} /LANG-GR
